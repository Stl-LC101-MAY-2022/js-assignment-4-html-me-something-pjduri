So how did I get into this?

I saw my first performance at a festival in 2003 and was immediately entranced. Actually learning to spin fire, however, remained on my to-do list for the better part of a decade.

It was totally by chance that I became Facebook friends with someone who was part of a troupe of fire performers, and one day he made a post that they were looking for some new members as well as to train a few more safety spotters. As an added bonus, I got to learn my first fire eating trick - extinguishing a torch in my mouth.

From there, I learned most everything else from a combination of YouTube and random encounters with peole who were willing to teach. Check out the rest of the page to find out more!

Safety Third (But Still on the List)

Let's face it. Sometimes it's fun to do something dangerous. "Safety First" would be to *not* put burning torches in my mouth.

But what's the fun in that?

So while we can enjoy dangerous things, we still don't want to be *completely* reckless, and dealing with fire is certainly no exception. Here are some of the things we do in order to be slightly less unsafe:

  • Mind what you're wearing:
    Polyester + fire = bad for the skin.
    Grease paint makeup is how beards ignite (speaking from personal experience.)
    Light, loose-fitting and flowy articles of clothing
  • Bring a friend: "Stop, drop and roll" is all well and good, but I'd rather have someone with a towel who knows how to put out a fire on a person.
  • FlameX for Natural Fibers: Yes, there's literally a product called FlameX. I use it to treat my safety towels. I have heard of performers using the synthetic fibers version to get away with wearing costumes they otherwise wouldn't, but I don't think I'd want to wear that stuff. It smells.
  • Learn from people who know: Seriously. Learning from YouTube is enough to pick up the movement, but have at least an experienced safety spotter present before attempting anything new with fire.

This list isn't exhaustive, and not every performer agrees on every particular safety measure, but the point is to not just light things on fire and go.

Then again, somebody did exactly that at some point, otherwise these arts would not exist.

Putting Burning Torches in my Mouth for Fun and (sometimes) Profit

Having learned how to use my mouth to extinguish a burning torch, the next thing I learned was vapor control. It's exactly as it sounds - I hold some fuel vapor in my mouth and slowly blow it out to make it look like I'm exhaling fire.

It isn't as flashy as the massive plumes of fire that breathers produce, but it's up close and personal with fire and I like it.

Getting Spinny With It!

A member of that first troupe made practice poi out of fabric and beanbags. Given how much more forgiving these were than the tennis balls, I started practicing again. Having learned some names, websites and YouTube channels where I could learn, I dove in, and I dove in hard. I practiced at bus stops. I practiced in the yard before and after work (much to the amusement of the roomies and neighbors). I practiced in the pool room at the hotel where I worked overnights.

I loved it. I could just trance out and go, with or without fire.

Must I pick a favorite?

People often ask me which I enjoy more. The answer is "yes."

Fire in the Future?

I've been on a hiatus from performing for some time now. It was just one of those things where life got in the way and I could no longer devote time and resources to burning. I've recently pulled my gear out and started seeing what I remember. It's not likely that I'll go out trying to get gigs again,
