Safety Third (But Still on the List)

Let's face it. Sometimes it's fun to do something dangerous. "Safety First" would be to *not* put burning torches in my mouth.

But what's the fun in that?

So while we can enjoy dangerous things, we still don't want to be *completely* reckless, and dealing with fire is certainly no exception. Here are some of the things we do in order to be slightly less unsafe:

  • Mind what you're wearing:
    Polyester + fire = bad for the skin.
    Grease paint makeup is how beards ignite (speaking from personal experience.)
    Light, loose-fitting and flowy articles of clothing ignight more easily.
  • Bring a friend: "Stop, drop and roll" is all well and good, but I'd rather have someone with a towel who knows how to put out a fire on a person.
  • FlameX for Natural Fibers: Yes, there's literally a product called FlameX. I use it to treat my safety towels. I have heard of performers using the synthetic fibers version to get away with wearing costumes they otherwise wouldn't, but I don't think I'd want to wear that stuff. It smells.
  • Learn from people who know: Seriously. Learning from YouTube is enough to pick up the movement, but have at least an experienced safety spotter present before attempting anything new with fire.

This list isn't exhaustive, and not every performer agrees on every particular safety measure, but the point is to not just light things on fire and go.

Then again, somebody did exactly that at some point, otherwise these arts would not exist.

Putting Burning Torches in my Mouth for Fun and (sometimes) Profit

Having learned how to use my mouth to extinguish a burning torch, the next thing I learned was vapor control. It's exactly as it sounds - I hold some fuel vapor in my mouth and slowly blow it out to make it look as though I'm slowly exhaling fire.

It isn't as flashy as the massive plumes of fire that breathers produce, but it's up close and personal with fire and I like it.

Getting Spinny With It!

A member of that first troupe made practice poi out of fabric and beanbags. Given how much more forgiving these were than the tennis balls, I started practicing again. Having learned some names, websites and YouTube channels where I could learn, I dove in, and I dove in hard.

I loved it. I could just trance out and go, with or without fire. I practiced at bus stops. I practiced in the yard before and after work (much to the amusement of the roomies and neighbors). I practiced in the pool room at the hotel where I worked overnights. I could not get enough.

Must I pick a favorite?

People often ask me which I enjoy more. The answer is "yes."

Fire in the Future?

It's not likely that I'll go back to doing this professionally. Ultimately, the business side of show business doesn't suit me and I'm already moving forward with other endeavors.

My hiatus from the art itself, however, is approaching its end. I've already started picking the guitar back up, and fire and flow are next in line.
